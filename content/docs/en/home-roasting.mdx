Roasting your own coffee closes the last gap between farm and cup. Green (unroasted) coffee beans are stable for 12–18 months when stored correctly; roasted coffee goes stale within weeks. Home roasting means your coffee is always at peak freshness — and it costs a fraction of buying roasted beans.

## Why Roast at Home?

- **Freshness** — you roast on demand, days before brewing, not weeks
- **Cost** — quality green beans typically cost 30–60% less than the equivalent roasted
- **Access** — green importers carry varieties and micro-lots that never reach retail as roasted coffee
- **Control** — you decide the roast level: light, medium, or dark

A **popcorn popper** is the cheapest entry point — many specialty roasters started with one. The key requirement: it must blow air from the bottom (not the side), so the beans agitate during roasting. Results are inconsistent but educational.

A **fluid bed roaster** (Fresh Roast SR800) is the most popular step up for home roasters: transparent chamber so you can watch the roast, reliable airflow control, good repeatability.

**Drum roasters** (Behmor, Gene Café, Hottop) mimic commercial roasters and produce the most consistent results with the most control, at higher cost.

## The Roast Curve

A coffee roast moves through distinct stages. Understanding them lets you intervene at the right moment.

### Stage 1 — Drying (0–5 min)

Green beans contain 10–12% moisture. The first minutes of roasting evaporate this water. Beans turn from green to pale yellow. The smell is grassy, then hay-like.

No flavour development is happening yet — this phase is purely drying.

### Stage 2 — Maillard Reaction (5–8 min)

As moisture drops and temperature rises, sugars and amino acids begin reacting (**Maillard reaction**). The beans brown gradually: yellow → cinnamon → light brown. Aromas shift from grassy to bready, caramel, and toast.

### Stage 3 — First Crack (~8–10 min)

The most important event in the roast. As internal pressure builds, the beans crack audibly — a popping sound resembling popcorn. **First crack marks the entry to light roast territory.**

At this point:
- The roast is technically drinkable
- The bean has expanded ~50% in volume
- Internal temperature is approximately 196°C

### Stage 4 — Development Phase

The time between the end of first crack and the end of roast is the **development phase**. This is where most flavor compounds form: acidity, sweetness, fruit notes, and body.

**Development Time Ratio (DTR)** = time in development ÷ total roast time.

| DTR | Result |
|-----|--------|
| &lt;15% | Underdeveloped — sour, grassy, raw |
| 20–25% | Optimal for most profiles — complex, balanced |
| &gt;30% | Overdeveloped — flat, baked, one-dimensional |

Most specialty roasters target 20–25% DTR.

### Stage 5 — Second Crack (~12–14 min)

As cell walls begin to fracture, a second, quieter cracking sound occurs. **Second crack marks medium-dark roast territory.** Oils begin to surface on the bean. If you continue past second crack, you are in dark roast / French roast territory — origin character disappears, replaced by roast character (smoke, char, bitterness).

For specialty coffee, most home roasters stop **between first crack and early second crack**.

## Cooling

**Cooling is not optional — it is part of the roast.** Beans retain heat and will continue to roast after the heat source is removed. You need to cool a batch from ~200°C to room temperature in **60–90 seconds**.

Methods:
- Pour between two colanders outdoors or over a sink while stirring vigorously
- Use a metal colander with a fan blowing through it
- Dedicated drum roasters often have a built-in cooling tray

Slow cooling = continued development = darker roast than intended = flat cup.

## Degassing

Freshly roasted coffee releases CO₂ for 24 hours to several days. This is degassing.

- Brew too soon and the escaping CO₂ disrupts extraction, producing uneven, often sour results
- **Wait at least 24–48 hours** after roasting before brewing filter coffee
- **Wait 72+ hours** before using fresh-roasted beans for espresso
- Peak flavour for most specialty coffee is **3–10 days** after roasting

<Callout type="tip">
  Store green beans in a sealed bag or airtight container, away from light and heat. Well-stored green coffee keeps for 12–18 months. Once roasted, consume within 2–4 weeks for peak flavour. Date your bags.
</Callout>

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Symptom | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| Stopping too early (underdeveloped) | Sour, grassy, raw taste | Extend development past first crack |
| Roasting too fast (baked) | Flat, dull, cereal-like | Slow your roast; preheat equipment longer |
| Cooling too slowly | Darker than intended, flat | Cool aggressively in &lt;90 seconds |
| Poor ventilation | Smoke, fire risk, chaff fires | Always roast near an open window or outdoor |
| Roasting old green beans | Papery, woody, lifeless | Use green beans &lt;18 months old |
| No airflow in popper | Uneven, scorched | Ensure air blows from bottom, not side |
