Kenya produces a relatively small volume of coffee — roughly 700,000 to 1 million 60 kg bags per year — but commands disproportionate influence in the specialty world. An exceptionally high percentage of Kenyan production reaches specialty grade, and top lots regularly score among the highest-rated coffees on the planet.

<Callout type="info">
  Kenya's SL28 and SL34 varieties, developed by Scott Agricultural Laboratories in the 1930s and 40s, are among the most celebrated coffee varieties in the world. Their distinctive blackcurrant and phosphoric acidity define the "Kenyan profile" that roasters globally pay premium prices to source.
</Callout>

## Geography & Climate

Kenya straddles the equator but grows most of its coffee on the **slopes of Mt. Kenya and the Aberdare Range** at altitudes between 1,400 and 2,100m. The high altitude, equatorial light, and volcanic red soils (Nitisols) combine to produce exceptional cherry density and sugar concentration.

Kenya benefits from **two rainy seasons**, which produce two harvests:
- **Main crop** — October to December (the larger, higher-quality harvest)
- **Fly crop** — June to August (smaller, lighter lots)

## The Factory System

Most Kenyan coffee is grown by **smallholder farmers** with 0.1–1 ha of land each. Farmers bring ripe cherries to **cooperative-owned wet mills**, confusingly called "factories." The factory processes the coffee for the whole community.

This means a Kenyan coffee labeled with a factory name (e.g., "Gichatha-ini") represents the combined work of hundreds or even thousands of small farmers. Factory reputation matters enormously — the best factories are meticulous about cherry selection and fermentation.

After processing, most coffee is traded through the **Nairobi Coffee Exchange** weekly auction. Specialty roasters can buy direct from cooperatives or through the auction system.

## Key Regions

### Nyeri

Often cited as Kenya's finest growing region. Located on the eastern slopes of Mt. Kenya at 1,500–2,100m.

**Flavor profile:** Intense blackcurrant, tomato, grapefruit, bright "juicy" acidity, complex finish. Nyeri coffees are among Kenya's most sought-after and consistently score 88–92+ SCA points.

Notable factories: Gichatha-ini, Karogoto, Tegu, Mahiga.

### Kirinyaga

The slopes directly below Mt. Kenya's peak. Similar altitude and climate to Nyeri with slightly more body.

**Flavor profile:** Berry, dried fruit, dark cherry, full body, structured acidity.

### Murang'a

South of Nyeri on the Aberdare Range. Slightly lower altitude but excellent quality.

**Flavor profile:** Red fruit, brown sugar, medium-high acidity, clean finish.

### Kiambu (Limuru & Thika)

Near Nairobi. Historically significant — Limuru was where Kenya's first coffee was planted. Altitude around 1,500–1,800m.

**Flavor profile:** Classic Kenyan profile but slightly softer than the Mt. Kenya regions.

### Embu & Meru

Eastern slopes of Mt. Kenya. Increasingly producing quality specialty lots.

**Flavor profile:** Tropical fruit, moderate acidity, full body.

## Varieties

| Variety | Notes |
|---------|-------|
| SL28 | The gold standard. Outstanding cup quality; blackcurrant and phosphoric acidity. Drought-resistant but susceptible to CBD and rust. |
| SL34 | High yield, excellent quality; performs well at higher altitudes. Blackcurrant, bright. |
| Ruiru 11 | Disease-resistant hybrid developed in the 1980s; widely planted but lower cup quality. Increasingly replaced by Batian. |
| Batian | Newer disease-resistant variety with significantly better cup quality than Ruiru 11. Increasingly common. |
| K7 | Older variety; mild, less complex but reliable. |

## Processing

Kenya uses an elaborate **wet (washed) process** that is distinctive in its detail:

1. Cherries are floated to remove unripes and sorted by density
2. Pulped the same day
3. Fermented in tanks for **24–72 hours** (wet fermentation) — longer than most washed origins
4. Washed thoroughly with clean water
5. **Soaked in fresh water for 16–24 hours** (the "double soak" or "Kenya wash") — this extra step is credited with the clarity and brightness of Kenyan cups
6. Dried on raised beds for 7–15 days

<Callout type="tip">
  When buying Kenyan coffee, look for the factory name rather than just "Kenya" on the label. Top factories like Gichatha-ini (Nyeri), Karogoto (Nyeri), and Ndumberi (Kiambu) command the highest prices at auction and consistently deliver the blackcurrant-forward profile that defines the origin.
</Callout>
