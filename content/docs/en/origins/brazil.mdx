Brazil is responsible for roughly **35–40% of all coffee produced globally**, making it the undisputed volume leader. More importantly for specialty drinkers, it also produces more specialty coffee by raw volume than any other country — the Cup of Excellence program was born here in 1999.

<Callout type="info">
  Brazil produces approximately 60–65 million 60 kg bags per year. Specialty-grade lots represent roughly 10–15% of that — still more volume than most countries produce in total.
</Callout>

## Geography & Climate

Unlike Colombia or Ethiopia, most Brazilian coffee grows at **relatively modest altitudes** — 700 to 1,400m. The terrain is largely flat to gently rolling, which enables large-scale mechanical strip harvesting. This makes Brazilian coffee more economically efficient to produce, but means cherries ripen somewhat faster with less complexity than ultra-high-altitude lots.

The climate is seasonal and dry during harvest (April–September), which is ideal for **natural (dry) processing** on large patios and raised beds. Brazil is the natural process heartland.

There is **one harvest per year**, unlike Colombia's two.

## Key Regions

### Sul de Minas

The largest and most diverse coffee-growing region in Brazil, spanning the southern highlands of Minas Gerais state at 900–1,200m.

**Flavor profile:** Milk chocolate, hazelnut, caramel, mild citric acidity, medium-full body. The "classic Brazilian" cup that most people picture.

Sul de Minas produces a huge range of lots from everyday commercial grades to exceptional micro-lots.

### Cerrado Mineiro

A distinct plateau region in western Minas Gerais. Brazil's **first protected geographic indication (GI) for coffee** (2005). The cerrado's flat landscape, well-defined wet and dry seasons, and consistent climate produce reliable, uniform quality.

Altitude: 800–1,100m.

**Flavor profile:** Chocolate, dried fruit, full body, low-medium acidity. Often very sweet from extended cherry maturation in the dry season.

### Mogiana

Straddling the border of Minas Gerais and São Paulo states at 900–1,300m. One of Brazil's oldest specialty regions.

**Flavor profile:** Dark chocolate, walnut, brown sugar, low acidity. Reliable, structured cups.

### Chapada Diamantina (Bahia)

A newer specialty region in Bahia state at 900–1,200m. The dramatic plateau landscape and slightly higher humidity produce more complex cups than the cerrado.

**Flavor profile:** Stone fruit, milk chocolate, moderate acidity — more nuanced than classic Sul de Minas lots.

### Espírito Santo

Brazil's second-largest producing state, known primarily for **Conilon** (Robusta). However, a growing number of specialty Arabica producers in the state's mountainous western areas (Montanhas do Espírito Santo) are producing distinctive lots.

## Varieties

| Variety | Notes |
|---------|-------|
| Yellow Bourbon | Brazil's most prized specialty variety; sweet, complex, with a distinctive yellow cherry. Limited production. |
| Mundo Novo | Typica × Bourbon cross; productive, balanced, the backbone of Sul de Minas |
| Catuaí (Red & Yellow) | Compact, high-yielding; widely planted; reliable quality |
| Icatu | Disease-resistant hybrid; used widely in Cerrado |
| Acaiá | Large bean, low acidity; often found in commercial lots |
| Arara | Newer variety; complex cup, increasingly found in specialty |
| Topázio | Bourbon descendant; sweet, caramel, sought-after in competitions |

## Processing

Brazil is the home of the **natural (dry) process** at scale — whole cherries are dried on patios or raised beds under the sun. The flat terrain and dry harvest season make this practical in a way it simply isn't in wetter, steeper origins.

Brazil also **invented pulped natural (honey) processing** in the 1990s, as producers sought a middle ground between the cleanness of washed lots and the fruit-forward character of naturals.

Fully washed ("lavado") lots exist and are growing in specialty, prized for their clean, bright cups — but they remain a minority.

<Callout type="tip">
  When buying Brazilian specialty coffee, look for the region on the label (Cerrado Mineiro, Sul de Minas, Mogiana). A "Yellow Bourbon" designation on a natural lot is a strong signal of quality — these fetch premium prices at Cup of Excellence auctions.
</Callout>
