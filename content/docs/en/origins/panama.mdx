Panama produces a tiny volume of coffee — around 100,000 to 200,000 60 kg bags per year — and yet it commands more column inches in specialty coffee writing than origins ten times its size. The reason is a single variety: **Gesha**. When Hacienda La Esmeralda won the 2004 Best of Panama competition with a Gesha lot that scored unlike anything judges had tasted before, it reset expectations for what a coffee cup could be.

<Callout type="info">
  At the 2021 Best of Panama auction, a washed Gesha lot from Hacienda La Esmeralda sold for $2,575 per pound — a world record at the time. Even entry-level specialty Panamanian Gesha routinely trades at 10–20× the price of top Colombian or Ethiopian lots.
</Callout>

## Geography & Climate

Panama's specialty coffee grows almost entirely in the **Chiriquí highlands** in the western part of the country, near the border with Costa Rica. The dominant feature is **Volcán Barú** — Panama's highest peak at 3,475m — an inactive volcano whose flanks provide the growing zones.

Growing altitudes: **1,200 to 1,800m** in Boquete (Pacific slope) and slightly lower in the Volcán-Renacimiento area (Atlantic-influenced slope).

The Pacific and Atlantic sides of the divide have meaningfully different climates. Boquete is drier and sunnier; the Atlantic side gets more rainfall, creating distinct cup profiles from the same varieties.

## Key Regions

### Boquete

The most famous coffee region in Panama. A mountain valley on the Pacific slope of Volcán Barú at 1,300–1,800m. Home to most of Panama's iconic farms.

The combination of volcanic soil, high altitude, and the cool mountain air of the Chiriquí highlands produces exceptional cherry development.

**Flavor profile:** Jasmine, bergamot, stone fruit (peach, apricot), tropical fruit, delicate tea-like body, refined acidity. When well-processed, Boquete Gesha cups are among the most distinctive and complex in the world.

Notable farms: Hacienda La Esmeralda, Kotowa, Carmen, Mama Cata.

### Volcán-Renacimiento

On the Atlantic-facing slope of Volcán Barú. Cooler, wetter, and more cloud-covered than Boquete, producing slightly different cup profiles.

**Flavor profile:** Similar Gesha florals, but often heavier body and more fruit intensity.

## The Gesha Story

Gesha (also spelled Geisha) was collected from wild forest in the Gori Gesha region of southwestern Ethiopia in 1931. It was introduced to the CATIE research station in Costa Rica in the 1950s and distributed to farms in Panama, where it languished for decades — farmers found it difficult to grow and low-yielding.

In the early 2000s, Price Peterson of Hacienda La Esmeralda noticed one plot producing particularly intense cherries. His family isolated the variety, processed it separately, and entered it in the 2004 Best of Panama competition — where it won by an unprecedented margin, scoring in the high 90s on the SCA scale.

The specialty world had never tasted anything like it: jasmine florals, bergamot, stone fruit, and a delicacy that resembled fine tea more than coffee. Within years, demand for Gesha exploded globally.

## Processing

Panama Gesha is processed across the full spectrum: washed, natural, and honey. Each processing method produces strikingly different expressions of the variety:

- **Washed Gesha** — maximum clarity, florals, bergamot tea character
- **Natural Gesha** — intense tropical fruit, mango, passionfruit, more body
- **Honey Gesha** — middle ground; stone fruit, sweetness, some florals

<Callout type="tip">
  If budget is a constraint, look for Panama Gesha from the Volcán or Renacimiento areas rather than Boquete's top farms — similar variety and quality at significantly lower prices. The "Best of Panama" lots from Hacienda La Esmeralda are for the record books; the broader Chiriquí production is exceptional but more accessible.
</Callout>
