Costa Rica punches above its weight in specialty coffee. A small country, it produces around 2 million 60 kg bags per year — but with an outsized reputation for quality, innovation in processing, and traceability. In 1988, Costa Rica became the **first country in the world to legally ban Robusta cultivation**, cementing its identity as an Arabica-only, quality-first origin.

<Callout type="info">
  Costa Rica pioneered the "micro-mill revolution" starting in the 2000s. Rather than selling cherries to large central beneficios (wet mills), small farms began building their own micro-mills. This gave individual farmers control over processing and the ability to offer traceable, single-farm lots — transforming the Costa Rican specialty market.
</Callout>

## Geography & Climate

Costa Rica's coffee grows in the volcanic highlands of the Central Cordillera at **1,200 to 1,900m**. Fertile volcanic soils, ample rainfall, and distinct wet and dry seasons create ideal growing conditions.

The country is narrow enough that both the Atlantic and Pacific sides influence the climate. Most coffee grows on the Pacific slope, where the dry season coincides with the harvest (October–February).

## Key Regions

### Tarrazú

Costa Rica's most celebrated region. Located in the mountains south of San José at **1,400 to 1,900m** — among the highest growing elevations in Central America.

**Flavor profile:** Bright citric acidity, stone fruit (peach, apricot), honey sweetness, medium body, clean finish. Tarrazú produces some of Central America's most complex and sought-after specialty lots.

The region's main village, San Marcos de Tarrazú, lends its name to one of Costa Rica's most recognised geographic designations.

### Central Valley

Surrounding San José, at 1,000–1,600m. Costa Rica's largest producing region by volume. Home to many of the country's established beneficios and co-operatives.

**Flavor profile:** Balanced, caramel, mild citric acidity, medium body. More accessible profile than Tarrazú.

### West Valley (Valle Occidental)

The Naranjo, Palmares, and Santa Ana areas at 1,200–1,800m. Known for producing highly regarded micro-lot coffees with excellent acidity.

**Flavor profile:** Fruit-forward, orange, apricot, floral, medium-high acidity.

Notable for the **Villa Sarchi** variety, a natural mutation that thrives at high altitude in this region.

### Tres Ríos

A small but historically prestigious region east of San José on volcanic slopes at 1,200–1,650m. Often called the "Bordeaux of Costa Rica."

**Flavor profile:** Elegant, wine-like acidity, dark fruit, medium-full body, fine finish.

## Varieties

| Variety | Notes |
|---------|-------|
| Catuaí | Most widely planted; compact, productive, reliable |
| Caturra | Common; bright, citric |
| Villa Sarchi | Local Caturra mutation from the West Valley; bright, excellent at high altitude |
| Typica | Traditional; lower yield, cleaner cup |
| Gesha | Grown by a small number of premium producers; exceptional when well-executed |
| H1 (Hybrid) | Disease-resistant hybrid crossing Timor and Caturra; growing in use |

## Processing: The Honey Wheel

Costa Rica is the **birthplace of modern honey processing** at scale. While Brazil invented pulped natural in the 1990s, Costa Rican micro-mills elevated it into a precision craft with a spectrum of honey levels:

| Honey Level | Mucilage Left | Drying Time | Flavor |
|-------------|--------------|-------------|--------|
| Yellow Honey | ~25% | 8–10 days (open, sunny) | Clean, mild sweetness |
| Red Honey | ~50% | 12–16 days (partial shade) | More fruit, more body |
| Black Honey | ~75–100% | 25–30 days (shade) | Intense fruit, wine, full body |

The mucilage (the sticky fruit layer left on the bean) ferments slowly during drying, contributing sweetness and complexity. More mucilage = more fruit character, more fermentation risk, longer drying.

<Callout type="tip">
  Black honey Costa Rican lots offer the most dramatic flavour departure from the washed baseline — they taste almost natural-processed without the "funky" fermentation notes. If you want to explore honey processing, a Costa Rican black honey from Tarrazú or the West Valley is an ideal starting point.
</Callout>
