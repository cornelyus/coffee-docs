Guatemala has been exporting coffee since the 1850s and remains one of Central America's most important specialty origins. Its diversity of volcanic mountain ranges, altitudes, and microclimates creates dramatically different regional profiles — from the chocolate-spice cups of Antigua to the citrus-bright lots of Huehuetenango.

<Callout type="info">
  Guatemala produces approximately 3–4 million 60 kg bags per year. ANACAFÉ (Asociación Nacional del Café) officially recognises eight distinct growing regions, each with a protected geographic identity.
</Callout>

## Geography & Climate

Guatemala's coffee grows primarily in the western and central highlands at **1,000 to over 2,000m**. The country sits on the Central American Volcanic Arc — three active volcanoes surround the Antigua Valley alone. This volcanic geology creates mineral-rich soils (pumice and ash) that contribute to distinctive sweetness and body.

Rainfall, humidity, and shade levels vary dramatically by region, producing what ANACAFÉ calls the most diverse range of microclimates of any single coffee-producing country.

There is **one main harvest per year**, typically November through March.

## Key Regions

### Antigua

The most internationally recognised Guatemalan origin. The valley sits at 1,500–1,700m, surrounded by three active volcanoes: **Agua, Fuego, and Acatenango**. Their pumice-rich ash soil gives Antigua coffees a distinctive minerality.

**Flavor profile:** Dark chocolate, brown spice (cinnamon, clove), dried fruit, medium body, mild-moderate acidity. Remarkably structured and consistent year to year.

Antigua is one of the most tightly regulated regions — only coffees grown within the valley qualify for the "Antigua" designation.

### Huehuetenango

Guatemala's northernmost and most remote growing region, at altitudes reaching **1,500 to 2,100m** — among the highest non-volcanic growing elevations in Central America. The Cuchumatanes mountain range (the largest non-volcanic massif in Central America) protects the region from cold winds, allowing coffee to thrive at extreme altitudes.

**Flavor profile:** Bright citric and malic acidity, stone fruit (peach, apricot), floral, wine-like, medium body. Some of Guatemala's most complex and dynamic specialty lots.

Huehuetenango is a source of particularly prized Cup of Excellence lots.

### Atitlán

The slopes surrounding **Lake Atitlán**, a volcanic crater lake at 1,600m. Three volcanoes border the lake. The lake moderates temperature and humidity, creating a unique growing environment.

**Flavor profile:** Fruit-forward, jasmine, dark berry, bright acidity, full body.

### Cobán

Located in Alta Verapaz, a **cloud forest** region of constant mist and humidity. Unlike the dry-season harvests of most Guatemalan regions, Cobán's near-permanent cloud cover requires careful drying management.

**Flavor profile:** Earthy, wine, spice, full body, muted acidity. Very distinct character from other Guatemalan regions.

### Acatenango Valley

Named for the Acatenango volcano. The high altitude (1,300–2,000m) and volcanic soil produce exceptional cups.

**Flavor profile:** Honey, jasmine, peach, bright acidity, silky body.

## Varieties

| Variety | Notes |
|---------|-------|
| Bourbon | Traditional, excellent quality; sweet and balanced |
| Typica | Oldest variety in Guatemala; clean, classic profile |
| Catuaí | Widely planted, compact, productive; reliable quality |
| Caturra | Common; bright, citric, good but less complex than Bourbon |
| Pache | Local Typica mutation; dense bean, good body, mild acidity |
| Pacamara | El Salvador cross grown increasingly in Guatemala; very large bean, complex, full body |
| Gesha | Grown in limited quantities in high-altitude farms; exceptional when well-executed |

## Processing

Guatemala is predominantly a **washed (wet) process** country. Farms typically pulp, ferment in open tanks for 24–48 hours, wash, and dry on patios or raised beds.

Natural and honey processing are growing, especially in Huehuetenango and Acatenango, as farmers seek differentiation in specialty markets.

<Callout type="tip">
  Antigua and Huehuetenango are Guatemala's two most distinct regional profiles — they taste almost like different countries. If you find a single-farm or micro-lot labeled "Huehuetenango," expect brightness and complexity; if it says "Antigua," expect chocolate, spice, and structure.
</Callout>
