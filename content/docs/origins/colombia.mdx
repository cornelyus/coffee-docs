Colombia is the world's third-largest coffee producer and the largest producer of washed Arabica. Its geography — two Andean mountain ranges running north to south, straddling the equator — creates an extraordinary diversity of growing conditions.

## Geography & Climate

Colombia's Andes split into three cordilleras (mountain ranges): Western, Central, and Eastern. Coffee grows on the slopes of all three, at altitudes from 1,200 to 2,300m.

The country straddles the equator, which means many regions have **two harvests per year** (main crop and fly crop) — a significant advantage over single-harvest origins.

<Callout type="info">
  Colombia produces ~12.5 million 60kg bags per year, second only to Brazil in the Americas. Nearly all of it is washed Arabica.
</Callout>

## Key Regions

### Huila

The most celebrated Colombian origin. Located in the southern Andes at 1,500–2,100m.

**Flavor profile:** Bright red fruit acidity (cherry, apple), caramel sweetness, medium-full body. Often complex and sweet.

Huila produces Colombia's most sought-after micro-lot coffees.

### Nariño

Southernmost major coffee region, bordering Ecuador. Very high altitude — up to 2,300m. Despite proximity to the equator, cool temperatures create slow ripening and concentrated sugars.

**Flavor profile:** High acidity, very sweet, tropical fruit, floral.

### Antioquia (Medellín)

The historical center of Colombian coffee. The FNC (Federación Nacional de Cafeteros) was founded in Medellín in 1927. Coffees tend toward the classic Colombian profile.

**Flavor profile:** Balanced, nutty, caramel, mild citric acidity.

### Tolima

Emerging region between Huila and Antioquia. High altitude, isolated farms.

**Flavor profile:** Similar to Huila — bright, fruity, sweet. Less commercially available but excellent quality.

### Cauca

High-altitude growing between Nariño and Huila.

**Flavor profile:** Citric brightness, floral, stone fruit.

## Varieties

| Variety | Notes |
|---------|-------|
| Caturra | Most widely planted; compact, productive, good quality |
| Colombia | Disease-resistant hybrid developed by FNC; reliable, less complex |
| Castillo | FNC hybrid; rust-resistant, widespread, somewhat polarizing in specialty |
| Bourbon | Traditional, excellent quality, less disease-resistant |
| Pink Bourbon | Rare, prized; intensely sweet, floral |
| Gesha | Grown increasingly in Huila and Nariño; very high prices |

## The FNC and Juan Valdez

The Federación Nacional de Cafeteros (FNC) is a cooperative representing ~540,000 coffee-growing families. They created the "Juan Valdez" marketing character in 1959 and the "100% Colombian Coffee" campaign, which helped establish Colombia as synonymous with quality coffee globally.

The FNC's Denominación de Origen (geographic indication) system protects regional identities like "Café de Nariño" and "Café de Huila."

## Processing

Colombia is primarily a **washed** coffee country. Natural and honey processing are growing in the specialty sector but remain a small fraction of production.

The typical process:
1. Hand-pick ripe red cherries
2. Pulp same day
3. Ferment in tanks (18–40 hours, wet)
4. Wash thoroughly
5. Dry on raised African beds or patios

<Callout type="tip">
  "Double fermentation" Colombian lots — fermented before and after pulping — are increasingly available and produce distinctive sweetness and complexity.
</Callout>
